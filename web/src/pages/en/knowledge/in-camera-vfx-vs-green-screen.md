When you plan a shoot, you often face one question: **green screen or LED wall?** Both replace real locations with digital environments — but they do it in fundamentally different ways. Here is when each method pays off.

## Green screen: flexible, but work in post

With green screen you film in front of a green surface; the environment is added later in post (keying & compositing).

**Strengths:** cheap to set up, very flexible, freely interchangeable backgrounds, low space requirements.
**Weaknesses:** the final look only appears weeks later; lighting and reflections have to be matched in post at great effort; green spill on hair, glass and shiny surfaces; nobody on set sees the finished image.

## In-camera VFX: the look is created on set

With **in-camera VFX**, the environment runs in real time on an **LED wall**. With camera tracking, the background reacts in a perspective-correct way to every camera move — the finished image is created directly in camera.

**Strengths:** real ambient light and believable reflections on people and objects; director and client see the result immediately; far less post; backdrop changes in minutes.
**Weaknesses:** higher day/studio cost than a bare green-screen setup; bound to the wall size; very wide camera moves need planning.

## The decisive difference: light & reflections

The biggest advantage of the LED wall is **physical**: the light of the virtual scene falls on the subject for real. A sunset skyline bathes the face in warm light; glasses reflect the actual environment. With green screen all of this has to be recreated — and often looks “pasted on.” For glass, paint, metal and water, in-camera VFX really shines.

## When does each make sense?

- **Green screen** still makes sense for large flat setups, very tight budgets, or scenes where the background will be freely designed later anyway (e.g. abstract graphic worlds).
- **In-camera VFX (LED wall)** pays off when the look has to be right on set (sign-offs, client present), with reflective materials, with several backdrops in one shoot day, and when post should stay lean.

## And the cost?

Green screen is cheaper to set up — but the cost shifts into post production. With the LED wall you pay more for the studio, but save on post, travel and rework. For productions with several setups or high quality demands, in-camera VFX is often the more predictable calculation in the end.

> **Bottom line:** green screen for maximum flexibility on a small set — in-camera VFX when light quality, speed and planning certainty matter.
